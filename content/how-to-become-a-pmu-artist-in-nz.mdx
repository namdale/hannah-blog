Permanent makeup is one of the fastest-growing beauty careers in New Zealand. Skilled PMU artists set their own hours, build loyal client bases, and charge premium rates for work that genuinely changes how people feel about themselves. But the path from beginner to booked-out artist is often unclear. This guide covers what it actually takes.

## What does a PMU artist do?

Permanent makeup artists perform semi-permanent cosmetic procedures: nano hairstroke brows, lip blush, eyeliner tattoo and scalp micropigmentation (SMP). Each treatment implants pigment into the skin using specialised machines and needles — closer to tattoo artistry than to traditional makeup, and requiring proper training in skin anatomy, hygiene and colour theory.

## Step 1 — Choose the right training

Not all courses are equal. When comparing academies, look for:

- **Small class sizes** with genuine hands-on practice, not just theory slides
- **Live model work** under supervision before you graduate
- **Nano hairstroke technique** — the current industry standard, finer and more realistic than older microblading
- **Ongoing mentorship** after the course ends, because your first year of real clients is when you need support most

## Step 2 — Understand the regulations

In New Zealand, PMU falls under local council health regulations for skin-piercing services. You will need to operate from registered premises, follow strict sterilisation protocols, and in most councils hold an appropriate operator licence. A good academy walks you through the compliance requirements for your region — ask about this before you enrol.

## Step 3 — Budget for your starter kit

Expect to invest in a quality PMU machine, needle cartridges, pigments, practice skins and hygiene supplies. Buying cheap equipment is the most common beginner mistake — inconsistent machines produce inconsistent strokes, and clients notice.

## Step 4 — Build your portfolio and your Instagram

Your Instagram feed is your shop window. Photograph every healed result (with client permission), post consistently, and document your training journey — clients love watching an artist grow. Before-and-after content of healed work builds more trust than anything else.

## How long until you earn?

Most graduates take their first paying clients within weeks of completing training, starting at model rates and increasing as their portfolio grows. Artists who treat it as a business — consistent marketing, follow-up care, referral incentives — typically reach a sustainable income within their first year.
